House Toolkit: Majority in the House, Minority in the Senate

A practical playbook to stop, prevent, or reverse harmful executive actions. Built for fast use by Members, staff, and the public.

Plain language Step by step Examples Pass or fail mapping Accountability hooks

Quick read

  • Goal: use House power to block or unwind bad policy with or without Senate support.
  • Method: combine oversight, spending controls, fast‑track laws, and time‑boxed floor tactics.
  • Backstops: automatic triggers and metrics that force action and keep faith with voters.

Speaker of the House quick toolkit

Control recognition and floor schedule

  • What it is: the Speaker decides who is recognized to speak and when items come up.
  • Use it to: prioritize oversight, resolutions, and spending riders that limit bad policy.
Example: put a privileged resolution or a spending limitation on the floor before other business when an agency defies the law.

Rules Committee leverage

  • What it is: shape special rules to allow or block amendments that tighten accountability.
  • Use it to: make Holman amendments and policy riders in order on appropriations bills.

Questions of privilege

  • What it is: privileged resolutions on the rights of the House or its investigations.
  • Use it to: bring contempt or enforcement measures straight to the floor.

These tools rest on House rules and precedents that give the Chair broad recognition and agenda control.

Core tools for this scenario

1) Appropriations riders and limits

  • What it is: add “no funds may be used for X” or “funds available only if Y” language to stop a policy.
  • Why it works: the Antideficiency Act and the power of the purse make unlawful spending risky for officials.
  • Feasibility: high in the House. Senate may resist, so attach riders to must‑pass bills or slice into focused bills.
  • Accountability hooks: require monthly public certifications to release funds and referrals to the Government Accountability Office if missing.
Example: “None of the funds for Program Z may be used unless the agency posts the legal basis and cost estimate, and the Comptroller General confirms receipt.”

2) Holman Rule targeted cuts

  • What it is: allow amendments in appropriations that cut a specific program or reduce a specific salary.
  • Use it to: surgically defund an office that is the engine of a harmful policy.
  • Guardrails: avoid personal punishment intent. Aim at duties and funding, not a named person for retribution.
Example: reduce the salary for an office that ignores statutory deadlines to one dollar until compliance is certified.

3) Congressional Review Act (CRA)

  • What it is: fast‑track joint resolution to nullify an agency rule. Limits Senate filibuster.
  • Use it when: the executive issues a rule or guidance that exceeds the law or harms the public.
  • Extra: use the “lookback” window to reach late‑issued rules from the end of the prior Congress.
Example: file multiple CRA disapprovals on bundled rules and force Senate votes with simple majority threshold.

4) Resolutions of inquiry

  • What it is: a simple House resolution demanding specific facts from the President or a Cabinet Secretary, with a 14 legislative day clock in committee.
  • Use it to: get documents quickly when agencies stall normal oversight requests.
  • If ignored: escalate to contempt or spending holds.
Example: demand all legal memoranda and cost models used to justify a policy change within 14 legislative days.

5) Impoundment Control Act and Antideficiency Act enforcement

  • What it is: laws that require the executive to spend money as Congress directed and prohibit spending without authority.
  • Use it to: challenge slow‑walking, withholding, or repurposing funds for policy reasons.
  • Flow: letter to Office of Management and Budget and agency → Government Accountability Office legal decision → riders and contempt if needed.
Example: when the executive withholds obligated funds for policy reasons, request a Government Accountability Office ruling and freeze the responsible account by rider until compliance.

6) War Powers Resolution and National Emergencies Act checks

  • What it is: fast‑track procedures to force votes to authorize or end hostilities and terminate national emergencies.
  • Use it to: unwind emergency‑based policy or unauthorized military action even with Senate resistance.
  • Pair with: funding riders that bar spending under an emergency after a set date unless Congress votes to continue.
Example: file a joint resolution to end an emergency that drives a regulation, while placing a date‑certain cutoff in appropriations unless Congress affirmatively extends the emergency.

7) Contempt and enforcement options

  • What it is: inherent contempt by the House, criminal contempt referrals, and civil enforcement suits to compel compliance with subpoenas.
  • Use it when: agencies refuse to testify or turn over documents and the public interest requires speed.
  • Modernization option: House rule to impose daily monetary penalties on non‑compliant officials until production, with due process.
Example: adopt a privileged resolution directing the Sergeant at Arms to serve notice of fines that automatically accrue every day the subpoena remains unmet.

8) Calendar Wednesday rescue

  • What it is: a seldom used rule that lets a committee call up a reported bill on a Wednesday when leadership is blocking it.
  • Use it to: force floor time for a narrowly crafted oversight or accountability bill.
Example: call up a targeted bill requiring public posting of apportionment schedules when the Rules Committee declines a rule.

Automatic triggers that do not need a new vote

  • Self‑executing riders: “Funds for Account A are unavailable after 30 days unless the agency publishes required data and notifies the Comptroller General.”
  • Certification gates: “No travel funds for Office B until the Inspector General certifies policy compliance.” Security exceptions are permitted by law and must be listed publicly.
  • Statutory fast clocks: if a rule is issued, a Congressional Review Act clock starts and forces a privilege window without needing leadership consent.
  • Automatic referral to Government Accountability Office: any missed reporting deadline automatically triggers a public legal inquiry by the Comptroller General, with results sent to the House.
Collusion scenario: if the executive and a federal court stonewall, the House can pair inherent contempt with spending gates that halt the specific policy engine. Add a mandatory floor vote on a narrow, time‑limited cut to keep unity and make the pressure public.

Measurements and bright‑line tests

  • Debt and deficit guardrail: no net increase to the on‑budget deficit in the 5‑year and 10‑year Congressional Budget Office windows, unless offset within three fiscal years in the same bill.
  • Tax fairness test: a tax bill fails if the average effective federal tax rate paid by the top ten percent falls below the rate paid by the middle quintile, using official distribution tables.
  • Benefit share test: at least 60 percent of net benefits must go to the bottom 60 percent of households for any refundable credit expansion.
  • No public funds for recreation: no appropriated funds for recreational fees or dues for senior officials. Security and official protection remain funded and reported separately. Require monthly disclosure of any official travel that includes recreational activities.

Pass or fail mapping inside the Evergreen Pact

This toolkit plugs into the Pact’s map so every action has a next step if it passes or fails.

Pass → follow‑through

  • Appropriations rider passes → post compliance dashboard within 7 days → schedule oversight hearing in 30 days → renew rider if needed.
  • CRA passes one chamber but not the other → pivot to rider plus litigation referrals and public reporting.

Fail → escalate

  • Rider fails in Senate → split into narrower accounts and attach to must‑pass, then use Calendar Wednesday to move a transparency bill.
  • Subpoena ignored → inherent contempt notice and fines → pair with account freeze in next continuing resolution.

Risks and how to manage them

  • Bill of attainder risk: avoid naming individuals for punishment purpose. Target duties and funding, not a person.
  • Separation of powers: spending limits are strong, but do not block core security protection. Use narrow, tailored text.
  • Durability: pair quick riders with permanent transparency or oversight statutes to lock in gains.
