Top Issue #1

Universal healthcare

Healthcare is a right. The U.S. is the only wealthy OECD nation without universal coverage, while spending among the most per person.

The picture (by the numbers)

  • Spending: ≈$12,500 per person annually—nearly 2× the OECD average.
  • Outcomes: Lower life expectancy and higher infant mortality than peer nations.
  • Access: Millions uninsured or underinsured; many delay or skip care due to cost.

Plan: cover everyone and realign policy around health

  • Automatic universal enrollment. A guaranteed national benefits floor for all; supplemental options can remain for add-ons.
  • Prescription drugs. Direct price negotiation, end patent gaming, and cap out-of-pocket costs for essential medicines.
  • Reproductive freedom. Keep medical decisions between patients and doctors.
  • Mental health parity. Enforce equal access, networks, and reimbursement.
  • Primary care first. Invest in clinics and workforce in underserved areas to catch problems early and locally.
  • Policy alignment for health. With health as the north star, policies for safer food, clean water, clean air, and stronger workplace protections follow.

How we’ll get there

  • Step 1: Pass a universal coverage bill with automatic enrollment and a defined national benefits floor.
  • Step 2: Expand Medicare drug negotiation authority; enact patent reform to curb evergreening.
  • Step 3: Fund primary care/mental health workforce and clinics via targeted grants; prioritize shortage areas.
  • Step 4: Tie food/water/air standards and workplace safety targets to national health goals so agencies row in the same direction.

Top Issue #2

Lowering the cost of living

Families are being squeezed by housing, groceries, childcare, and utilities. Relief must be real and lasting—without raising taxes on working people.

What’s driving the squeeze

  • Housing: Limited supply and slow permitting push up prices and rents.
  • Groceries: Consolidation among processors/retailers weakens competition and price transparency.
  • Childcare: Costs rival college tuition for many families.
  • Utilities: Energy bills and home heating costs climbed in recent years.

Plan: more homes, fair markets, smaller monthly bills

  • Housing supply & permitting speed—no new taxes.
    • Federal incentives for states/locals to streamline permitting timelines with modern digital approvals and clear SLAs.
    • Back zoning flexibility near jobs and transit to add “missing middle” housing.
    • Expand support for modular/industrialized construction to cut time and cost.
    • Prioritize infrastructure grants for communities that meet predictable permit SLAs.
  • First-home affordability. Federally backed ~3% fixed-rate mortgages for first-time, income-eligible buyers; down-payment help tied to local costs.
  • Groceries. Enforce antitrust laws; require transparent fees and contracts; strengthen regional food hubs; targeted farm supports that stabilize prices while protecting small/mid-size farms.
  • Childcare. Expand/refund the Child and Dependent Care Tax Credit; scale supply grants so providers can add seats without lowering quality.
  • Lower monthly bills. Bigger rebates for weatherization, heat pumps, and community solar; streamline LIHEAP enrollment.
  • Reduce barriers to entry. Reform excessive licensing/certification costs; recognize equivalent experience; expand apprenticeships and paid earn-while-you-learn pathways.

How we’ll get there

  • Step 1: Pass a Housing Supply & Permitting Modernization Act that unlocks federal incentives for faster, digital permitting with transparent SLAs.
  • Step 2: Create a First-Home Mortgage Facility to deliver ~3% fixed rates for income-eligible first-time buyers.
  • Step 3: Direct DOJ/FTC to prioritize food supply chain competition; require standardized grocery fee/contract disclosures.
  • Step 4: Expand energy-savings rebates and community solar access; simplify eligibility and auto-enroll where possible.
  • Step 5: Launch a federal “Open Doors to Work” initiative to prune unnecessary licenses and scale apprenticeships with local employers.
Top Issue #3

End corruption & special interests

Politics should serve people, not donors. I will not take corporate PAC money—ever. Let’s end pay-to-play and put voters first.

Plan: clean elections, real transparency, strong enforcement

  • Ban corporate PACs and registered lobbyists from donating to or coordinating with campaigns; ban lobbyist bundling.
  • Public financing with democracy vouchers. One voucher per voter; campaigns rely on small donations + vouchers only.
  • Near real-time disclosure. Every campaign dollar reported within days, machine-readable, with strong penalties for violations.
  • Close the dark-money spigot. Robust beneficial-ownership and pass-through disclosure for political spending.
  • Stronger FEC. Balanced quorum rules, faster enforcement timelines, and civil/criminal referral authority with teeth.

How we’ll get there

  • Step 1: Introduce a Clean Campaigns Act that bans corporate PAC donations and lobbyist bundling; mandate near real-time reporting.
  • Step 2: Pilot a federal Voter Voucher program modeled on Seattle’s, expanding nationally as states opt in.
  • Step 3: Pass dark-money transparency standards tied to IRS and SEC reporting; require beneficial-owner disclosure for political spend.
  • Step 4: Modernize the FEC: clarify coordination rules, set swift penalties, and streamline referrals to DOJ for willful misconduct.

Fair taxation

Close loopholes so working families don’t subsidize the largest corporations and the ultra-wealthy.

Plan: simple, enforceable, pro-worker

  • Corporate minimum tax on book profits. If you report it to shareholders, you pay it.
  • Anti-offshoring rules. Align rates/treatment of foreign profits to end shell games and profit shifting.
  • Targeted relief. Expand the Child Tax Credit and EITC; simplify small-business expensing and compliance.
  • Near real-time transparency. Modernized e-filing and public dashboards for large filers.

Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) Tax — a fairness backstop

Make exploitation more expensive than fairness; reward firms that treat workers and communities well.

  • Triggers: extreme CEO-to-worker pay ratios (a CEO shouldn’t make in a day what an employee makes in a year), chronic wage theft, repeat OSHA violations, layoffs during record profits, and repeat environmental harm.
  • Mechanics: a surtax that scales with harm and automatically phases down as firms improve practices.
  • Disclosure: standardized reporting on pay ratios, injury rates, and community impacts.

Corporate accountability

Good jobs and safe workplaces should be the norm. Responsible companies should never be undercut by bad actors.

Work, pay, and safety

  • Stronger penalties for wage theft and repeat OSHA violators; prioritize criminal enforcement for egregious harm.
  • Incentives for firms that create living-wage U.S. jobs, invest in training, and demonstrate low injury rates.
  • Transparency on CEO-to-median worker pay, injury rates, and community impacts—shared in a common, comparable format.

Private equity and local communities

  • Address debt-loading models that strip assets, slash jobs/benefits, and leave communities with closures.
  • Condition favorable tax treatment on employment stability, pension protection, and post-acquisition safety outcomes.

Open more paths to good jobs

  • Reduce barriers to entry: reform excessive licensing/certification costs; accept equivalent experience where safe; expand apprenticeships and paid earn-while-you-learn pathways.
  • Support community colleges and trades programs aligned to local employer demand.

Link to the CSR Tax

The CSR Tax ties consequences to harmful practices and rewards firms that raise wages, cut injuries, narrow pay gaps, and invest in people.
