Top Issue #1

Universal healthcare

Healthcare is a fundamental right. Yet the United States stands alone among wealthy OECD nations in lacking universal coverage, despite spending more per person on healthcare than any of its peers.

The U.S. Healthcare System, By the Numbers

  • Spending: about $13,000 per person in 2022. That is nearly 2x the OECD average.
  • Outcomes: lower life expectancy and higher infant mortality than peer nations.
  • Access: uninsured reached record lows in 2023 then rose in 2024 with Medicaid unwinding. Millions remain uninsured or underinsured.

Plan: cover everyone and align policy with health

  • Automatic universal enrollment. A national benefits floor for all. Supplemental options can remain for add-ons.
  • Prescription drugs. Expand Medicare price negotiation. End patent gaming. Cap out-of-pocket costs for essential medicines at $2,000 per year for Part D enrollees starting 2025 and seek similar protections broadly.
  • Reproductive freedom. Keep medical decisions between patients and doctors.
  • Mental health parity. Enforce equal access, networks, and reimbursement.
  • Primary care first. Invest in clinics and workforce in underserved areas to catch problems early and locally.
  • Health in all policies. Use national health goals to steer food, water, air, and workplace rules in the same direction.

How we will get there

  • Step 1: Pass universal coverage with automatic enrollment and a defined national benefits floor.
  • Step 2: Expand drug negotiation authority and tighten patent rules to curb evergreening.
  • Step 3: Target grants to primary care and mental health clinics and training programs in shortage areas.
  • Step 4: Tie food, water, air, and workplace safety targets to national health metrics across agencies.

Top Issue #2

Lowering the cost of living

Families are under financial strain from housing and groceries to childcare and utilities. Relief must be real, lasting, and meaningful. No new taxes on working people.

What is driving the strain

  • Housing: limited supply and slow permitting push up the price to rent.
  • Groceries: consolidation and opaque fees weaken competition and transparency.
  • Childcare: costs rival in-state college tuition in many states.
  • Utilities: energy bills remain elevated compared with pre 2020 levels.

Plan: more homes, fair markets, smaller monthly bills

  • Housing supply and permitting speed.
    • Federal incentives for states and localities to streamline permitting timelines with modern digital approvals and clear service level targets.
    • Support zoning flexibility near jobs and transit to add missing middle housing.
    • Expand support for modular and industrialized construction to cut time and cost.
    • Prioritize infrastructure grants for communities that publish and meet predictable permit targets.
  • First home affordability. Create a First Home Mortgage Facility based on proven state housing finance models to buy down rates toward about 3 percent for income eligible first time buyers. Make the subsidy explicit and budgeted. Pair with down payment help that reflects local costs.
  • Groceries. Enforce antitrust laws. Require standardized disclosure of fees and contracts across the supply chain. Strengthen regional food hubs and targeted farm supports that stabilize prices while protecting small and mid size farms.
  • Childcare. Expand and refund the Child and Dependent Care Tax Credit. Use supply grants so providers can add seats without lowering quality.
  • Lower monthly bills. Bigger rebates for weatherization, heat pumps, and community solar. Streamline LIHEAP enrollment and use data matching for auto enrollment where possible.
  • Reduce barriers to entry. Prune excessive licensing and certification costs. Recognize equivalent experience where safe. Expand apprenticeships and paid earn while you learn pathways.

How we will get there

  • Step 1: Pass a Housing Supply and Permitting Modernization Act that unlocks federal incentives for faster, digital permitting with transparent targets.
  • Step 2: Stand up the First Home Mortgage Facility and scale it with federal credit support and bonds.
  • Step 3: Direct DOJ and FTC to prioritize food supply chain competition and require standardized grocery fee and contract disclosures.
  • Step 4: Expand energy savings rebates and community solar access with simplified eligibility and automatic enrollment where data allows.
  • Step 5: Launch a federal Open Doors to Work initiative that partners with states to right size licenses and scale apprenticeships with local employers.
Top Issue #3

End corruption and special interests

Politics should serve people, not donors. I will not take corporate PAC money. Let us end pay to play and put voters first.

Plan: clean elections, real transparency, strong enforcement

  • Ban corporate PAC and lobbyist contributions to campaigns and ban lobbyist bundling. Tighten coordination rules.
  • Public financing with voter vouchers. One voucher per voter. Campaigns rely on small donations plus vouchers.
  • Near real time disclosure. Every campaign dollar reported within days in machine readable form with strong penalties.
  • Close dark money loopholes. Beneficial ownership and pass through disclosure for political spending.
  • Stronger FEC. Balanced quorum rules, faster enforcement timelines, and clear referral authority for willful violations.

How we will get there

  • Step 1: Introduce a Clean Campaigns Act that bans corporate PAC donations and lobbyist bundling and mandates near real time reporting.
  • Step 2: Pilot a federal voter voucher program modeled on Seattle with state opt in.
  • Step 3: Tie disclosure rules to IRS and SEC filings and require beneficial owner reporting for political spend.
  • Step 4: Modernize the FEC with clear coordination rules, swift penalties, and streamlined referrals to DOJ for willful misconduct.

Fair taxation

Close loopholes so working families do not subsidize the largest corporations and the ultra wealthy.

Plan: simple, enforceable, pro worker

  • Strengthen the corporate minimum tax on book profits. If you report it to shareholders you pay it.
  • Anti offshoring rules. Align treatment of foreign profits to end shell games and profit shifting.
  • Targeted relief. Expand the Child Tax Credit and EITC. Simplify small business expensing and compliance.
  • Near real time transparency. Modernized electronic filing and public dashboards for large filers.

Corporate Social Responsibility Tax

Make exploitation more expensive than fairness. Reward firms that treat workers and communities well.

  • Triggers: extreme CEO to worker pay ratios, chronic wage theft, repeat OSHA violations, layoffs during record profits, and repeat environmental harm.
  • Mechanics: a surtax that scales with harm and phases down as firms improve practices.
  • Disclosure: standardized reporting on pay ratios, injury rates, and community impacts.

Corporate accountability

Good jobs and safe workplaces should be the norm. Responsible companies should never be undercut by bad actors.

Work, pay, and safety

  • Stronger penalties for wage theft and repeat OSHA violators. Use criminal referrals for egregious harm.
  • Incentives for firms that create living wage jobs, invest in training, and demonstrate low injury rates.
  • Transparency on CEO to median worker pay, injury rates, and community impacts in a common comparable format.

Private equity and local communities

  • Address debt loading models that strip assets, slash jobs and benefits, and leave communities with closures.
  • Condition favorable tax treatment on employment stability, pension protection, and post acquisition safety outcomes.

Open more paths to good jobs

  • Reduce barriers to entry. Right size licensing and certification costs. Accept equivalent experience where safe. Expand apprenticeships and paid earn while you learn pathways.
  • Support community colleges and trades programs aligned to local employer demand.

Link to the CSR Tax

The CSR Tax ties consequences to harmful practices and rewards firms that raise wages, cut injuries, narrow pay gaps, and invest in people.
