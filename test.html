House Toolkit: Majority in the House, Minority in the Senate

Use House power to stop, prevent, or reverse harmful executive actions even when the Senate is a roadblock.

Plain language Acronyms spelled out Examples Pass or fail mapping Measurable guardrails

Quick read

  • Start with spending controls, fast review votes, and public oversight.
  • Backstop with automatic triggers that do not need a second vote later.
  • Track results with clear measurements, not vibes.

Speaker of the House quick toolkit

Floor recognition and schedule

  • What it is: the Speaker controls who is recognized and when items come up.
  • Use it to: put accountability items first, hold slow‑walkers last.
Example: schedule a vote on a funding limit before any other business when an agency ignores a document request.

Rules Committee planning

  • What it is: a special rule can allow or block amendments.
  • Use it to: make accountability amendments in order and block poison pills.
Example: the rule for a spending bill allows a “no funds may be used” amendment that targets one harmful policy.

Questions of privilege

  • What it is: a privileged resolution on House rights or investigations.
  • Use it to: bring contempt or production demands straight to the floor.
Example: a resolution that orders the production of records and sets a deadline, with penalties if missed.

Core tools for this scenario

1) Appropriations riders and limits

  • Plain talk: add text in a funding bill that blocks money for a bad policy.
  • Why it bites: if money is blocked, the policy stalls in place.
  • How to aim it: name the activity, office, or account, and say what must be published to unlock funds.
Example: “No funds may be used to enforce Policy A until the legal memo and cost model are posted for public review.”

2) Holman Rule targeted cuts

  • Plain talk: reduce or zero out a program or a salary inside a funding bill.
  • When to use: when one office is the engine for the harm.
  • Guardrail: target duties and dollars, not a person for punishment.
Example: “Reduce the salary for the non‑compliance officer to one dollar until Policy A is withdrawn.”

3) Congressional Review Act (CRA)

  • Plain talk: a fast joint vote to cancel a rule. Senate debate is limited.
  • Even if it fails: the vote defines the issue and sets up a later reversal.
  • Side move: the “lookback” window can reach late‑term rules issued at the end of a Congress.
Example: file CRA resolutions for three related rules at once and demand separate up or down votes.

4) Resolutions of inquiry

  • Plain talk: a simple House resolution that demands facts from an agency on a fast clock.
  • When to use: when normal letters are ignored or delayed.
  • Next step: if the deadline is missed, move to fines or to a funding freeze.
Example: require delivery of all models and emails used to create a rule within 14 legislative days.

5) Spending law enforcement

  • Plain talk: the executive must spend money as directed and must not spend without authority.
  • When to use: if funds are withheld for policy reasons or rerouted.
  • Flow: demand records → request a legal review → block funds until fixed.
Example: freeze an office budget in the next bill until the missing spending plan is delivered.

6) War Powers and emergency checks

  • Plain talk: force votes to end an unauthorized action or an emergency that drives a policy.
  • Pair with: a funding cutoff date unless Congress votes to continue.
Example: file a resolution to end an emergency and include a date certain that blocks spending tied to that emergency after 30 days.

7) Contempt and compliance

  • Plain talk: if an agency refuses to comply, use fines, court action, or other penalties to compel action.
  • House option: adopt a rule that imposes daily monetary penalties on officials who defy subpoenas after due process.
Example: a privileged resolution that starts daily fines until documents are produced, while a funding rider blocks travel for the office that is in defiance.

8) Calendar Wednesday rescue

  • Plain talk: a rarely used path that lets a committee call up a reported bill on a Wednesday when leadership blocks it.
  • Use it for: narrow transparency or compliance bills that deserve daylight.
Example: bring up a short bill that requires public posting of spending plans within seven days.

Automatic triggers that do not need a new vote

  • Self‑executing gates: funds stay locked unless the agency posts required data and notifies the House clerk.
  • Certification switches: no travel or training funds for an office until the Inspector General certifies compliance.
  • Clock starts now: a rule submission starts a fast review window without leadership permission.
  • Auto referral: missed deadlines trigger a public legal review and a report to the House.
Collusion scenario: if agencies, lawyers, and a court all slow roll, pair a fines rule with a small but visible account freeze. Public dashboards show the missed steps and the money path that is blocked.

Measurements and bright‑line tests

  • Deficit guardrail: no increase to the on‑budget deficit in the five year and ten year windows under official scoring, unless fully offset within three fiscal years in the same bill.
  • Tax fairness test: the average effective federal tax rate for the top ten percent must not fall below the rate for the middle quintile in official tables.
  • Benefit share test: at least sixty percent of net benefits must go to the bottom sixty percent of households.
  • No recreation spending by officials: no public funds for club dues, greens fees, or similar recreation for senior officials. Security and protection are funded and reported separately.
  • Transparency timers: documents due in fourteen legislative days. Missed deadlines escalate to fines and a small account freeze in the next funding bill.

Pass or fail mapping inside the Evergreen Pact

Every step leads somewhere. If a vote passes, follow through. If it fails, escalate.

Pass path

  • Rider passes → post a public tracker within seven days and hold an oversight check at day thirty.
  • CRA passes one chamber → publish the vote record, turn to enforcement riders, and support court review with public records.
  • Documents delivered → close the loop with a short memo and remove the gate next cycle.

Fail path

  • Rider blocked in the Senate → split it into narrower accounts and attach to the next must‑pass item.
  • Subpoena ignored → begin daily fines after due process and freeze a travel or training account in the next bill.
  • Emergency vote fails → time‑limit the spending tied to that emergency and force a second vote on a short fuse.

Risks and guardrails

  • Precision matters: name the activity and the account. Avoid broad language that sweeps up unrelated work.
  • No personal punishment: target duties and dollars. Do not draft to punish a person.
  • Durability: pair fast riders with a short transparency law so the public can track compliance next year.
