Senate Rule XIV — Calendar Placement

A quick route to place a bill directly on the Senate calendar without referral through a slow committee.

Targeted Enforceable Fair Process

At a Glance

  • What it is: A procedure to have a bill read twice and placed on the calendar.
  • Why it helps: Creates a live vehicle for a motion to proceed and negotiations.
  • Safeguards: Coordination with floor staff to avoid procedural pitfalls.
  • Who is covered: Senators and floor managers.

When to Use

  • Committee delay: A bill will not move through regular order fast enough.
  • Negotiation leverage: You need a calendar vehicle to prompt talks.
  • Floor window: There is an opening for a motion to proceed.
Explanation: Choose this tool when the listed conditions match your goal and other routes are too slow or too broad.

How it Works

  1. Introduce: A Senator introduces the bill in the Senate and has it read twice.
  2. Object to referral: The bill is placed on the calendar under Rule Fourteen.
  3. Coordinate: Work with leadership on timing for a motion to proceed.
  4. Communicate: Use the calendar listing to build support.
Explanation: These steps focus on speed, clarity, and a documented record the public can follow.

Simple Examples

  • Calendar vehicle: Place S.___ on the calendar under Senate Rule Fourteen for use with a later motion to proceed.
  • Companion strategy: Calendar a House-passed bill to speed bicameral negotiations.
  • Spotlight: Use calendar status to drive public attention on a stalled policy.
Explanation: Examples show narrow, real world uses you can adapt quickly.

Risks and Mitigations

  • No guarantee of floor time: This is a parking spot, not a fast track.
  • Political costs: Overuse can frustrate committee chairs.
  • Timing risks: Coordinate closely with floor managers.
Explanation: Mitigations keep the tool fair and effective while reducing conflict and delay.

Copy Ready Floor Language

I ask that the bill be read the second time and placed on the calendar under Rule Fourteen. I object to further proceedings to which the reading of the bill may be referred.
