Subpoena Daily Fines

A House rule or statute that imposes a civil fine for each day a lawful subpoena is ignored after a short cure period, with court review and clear collection.

Targeted Enforceable Fair Process

At a Glance

  • What it is: Daily civil penalties that begin after a missed return date and cure notice.
  • Why it helps: Converts delay into cost the very next day.
  • Safeguards: Written notice, chance to cure, court review, and publication.
  • Who is covered: Executive officials, companies, and other witnesses under subpoena.

When to Use

  • Missed return date: The subpoena deadline passed without substantial compliance.
  • Time sensitive: Delay risks safety, funds, or legal deadlines.
  • Other tools too slow: Criminal contempt or referrals will not move fast enough.
Explanation: Choose this tool when the listed conditions match your goal and other routes are too slow or too broad.

How it Works

  1. Notice: The committee issues a written notice that lists what is missing and sets a cure date.
  2. Trigger: If there is no substantial compliance by the cure date, a daily fine begins the next day.
  3. Court lane: The committee may apply to the United States District Court for the District of Columbia for confirmation and review.
  4. Collection: The Department of the Treasury collects. Courts may order escrow or a limited asset freeze.
  5. Stop rule: Fines stop on substantial compliance or court relief.
Explanation: These steps focus on speed, clarity, and a documented record the public can follow.

Simple Examples

  • Executive emails: Five thousand dollars per day until a defined set of emails is produced or a judge rules on privilege.
  • Corporate records: Twenty five thousand dollars per day until a grant compliance file is produced.
  • Deposition: A witness appears within seven days after fines begin.
Explanation: Examples show narrow, real world uses you can adapt quickly.

Risks and Mitigations

  • Overreach claims: Use narrow subpoenas and detailed findings.
  • Privilege disputes: Require a privilege log and allow in camera review.
  • Collection logistics: Pair fines with escrow or a limited asset freeze.
Explanation: Mitigations keep the tool fair and effective while reducing conflict and delay.

Copy Ready Rule Text

Rule X. Subpoena Compliance and Daily Civil Penalties (a) Notice. When a subpoena return date passes without substantial compliance, the issuing committee shall provide written notice that identifies the items that remain due and a cure date no less than three calendar days after the notice. (b) Penalty. If substantial compliance is not achieved by the cure date, a civil penalty shall accrue for each calendar day of non compliance beginning on the day after the cure date and ending on substantial compliance or on a court order that finds a lawful excuse. (c) Amount. For senior executive officials and corporate entities, up to twenty five thousand dollars per day. For other witnesses, up to five thousand dollars per day. The amount shall be stated in the notice and is subject to review by a court for reasonableness. (d) Court review. The committee may apply to the United States District Court for the District of Columbia for an order that confirms the duty to comply and the daily penalty schedule. The court may modify or pause the penalty if the witness demonstrates a good reason for delay. (e) Collection. Penalties are debts owed to the United States. The Department of the Treasury shall collect. The court may order escrow or an asset freeze limited to the likely penalty. The court may award reasonable attorneys' fees to a prevailing party in the House of Representatives. (f) Publication. The Clerk shall maintain a public docket that lists each notice, cure date, and collection action under this rule.
