Impeachment Continuity and Accountability Act (2027)

Guardrails that keep government steady during impeachment. Protect continuity of operations. Clean up spending rules. Require fast approval for any new emergency. Preserve records and strengthen communications integrity. Enforced by independent oversight and quick court review.

TITLE I — CONTINUITY OF GOVERNMENT AND OPERATIONS

SEC. 101. Definitions. Impeachment period.

The impeachment period runs from the vote in the House of Representatives to adopt articles of impeachment until the final verdict by the Senate. It also ends on dismissal or after ninety days with no action by the Senate. A major action includes a new emergency declaration. It also includes a new executive order with large budget or legal effect. It includes a rule marked as major. It includes a transfer above agency thresholds. It includes mass personnel actions that affect one hundred or more career employees or twenty or more members of the Senior Executive Service.

Explanation: Sets a clear clock. Lists the actions that are too large to move forward without added checks.

SEC. 102. Continuity and acting service rules.

Clarifies how acting officials are named. Stops repeat temporary appointments used to avoid Senate confirmation. Keeps mission essential operations lists on file with the Office of Management and Budget and the Office of Personnel Management. Bans structural reorganizations without a law that allows them.

Explanation: Prevents workarounds that avoid the Senate. Keeps core services steady. Stops large reshuffles that lack a law.

SEC. 103. Essential services protection.

Requires continuity plans for public safety. Requires plans for care for veterans. Requires plans for Social Security and Medicare operations. Requires plans for critical infrastructure and national security support. The Government Accountability Office and agency inspectors general perform readiness checks.

Explanation: Protects the programs people rely on. Independent offices verify readiness.

TITLE II — IMPLEMENTATION SAFEGUARDS DURING IMPEACHMENT

SEC. 201. Funds defined broadly.

For this title, funds include dollars. Funds include supplies. Funds include staff time. Funds include information technology services. Funds include the use of federal buildings and other facilities.

Explanation: Stops end runs that use non cash resources to implement actions.

SEC. 202. Certifications. Oversight lanes. Quick court review.

Before a major action during an impeachment period, the head of the agency must certify legal authority. The head must certify need and cost impact. The certification goes to the Government Accountability Office. It goes to the relevant inspector general. It goes to the Office of Management and Budget and to Congress. The action may be challenged on a quick schedule in the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia. Courts may issue orders to stop the action.

Explanation: Requires signed proof before big moves. Sends notice to independent offices and to Congress. Lets courts step in fast.

SEC. 203. Suspension of major rules without certification.

Major rules published during an impeachment period are paused unless the certifications described in section 202 are complete. Life safety and court ordered compliance are exempt.

Explanation: Big rules stop unless the paperwork is done. Urgent safety and court orders go forward.

TITLE III — EMERGENCY POWERS APPROVAL TO OBLIGATE

SEC. 301. Approval requirement.

No funds may be obligated or spent to carry out a new emergency declaration that begins during an impeachment period unless a joint resolution of approval is enacted within seven legislative days after Congress receives notice.

Explanation: New emergencies during impeachment need a quick and public yes vote to spend money.

SEC. 302. Carve outs.

Immediate force protection. Life saving actions. Disaster response that is already underway. Court ordered compliance. These may proceed for up to seven days based on written certifications. They stop unless approval is enacted.

Explanation: Urgent work continues. Congress must approve to go beyond a short window.

SEC. 303. Fast track procedures.

Any Member may introduce an approval resolution. The resolution receives priority. Debate is limited. There are no delay tactics. There is a guaranteed yes or no vote on the floor of each chamber within seven legislative days.

Explanation: Prevents stalling. Forces a quick and clear vote.

SEC. 304. Pilot and sunset.

This applies across the government for two years after the law is enacted. A report is due at eighteen months on results and on recommended permanent standards.

Explanation: Tests the tool. Keeps what works. Ends what does not.

TITLE IV — COMMUNICATIONS AND PUBLICATION INTEGRITY

SEC. 401. Publication timelines.

The Office of the Federal Register and the Government Publishing Office must publish executive actions promptly. Improper delays are reportable to the Government Accountability Office. Agencies must provide machine readable texts. Agencies must provide legal authority citations and cost statements.

Explanation: Public records must be timely and complete. People can see and search them.

SEC. 402. Records preservation and disclosure.

Requires the preservation of Presidential and agency records related to major actions. This includes electronic messages and encrypted channels. Creates a litigation hold during the impeachment period. Speeds up processing under the Freedom of Information Act for covered materials.

Explanation: Protects the paper trail and the digital trail. Speeds up public access.

SEC. 403. Transparency index for controlling legal and regulatory determinations.

Requires an indexed public summary of controlling opinions by the Office of Legal Counsel and controlling determinations by the Office of Information and Regulatory Affairs that are relied upon for major actions. The index includes dates, legal authorities, and scope. A classified annex is allowed when needed.

Explanation: Shows the legal building blocks behind major actions without exposing secure details.

TITLE V — OVERSIGHT, ENFORCEMENT, AND WHISTLEBLOWERS

SEC. 501. Independent oversight.

The Government Accountability Office conducts quarterly audits of certifications, emergency approvals, and publication compliance. Inspectors general receive dedicated hotlines and may issue immediate compliance directives.

Explanation: Regular checks by independent offices. Clear lanes to fix problems.

SEC. 502. Beneficiary and congressional enforcement.

Allows suits by affected parties and by committees to stop violations of Titles Two through Four. Exclusive venue is the United States District Court for the District of Columbia. The schedule targets fourteen days for briefing and seven days for a decision. Direct appeal goes to the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia.

Explanation: People who are harmed and congressional committees can go to court quickly and in one place.

SEC. 503. Whistleblower and inspector general shields.

Strengthens timelines at the Office of Special Counsel. Expands protection against retaliation for disclosures related to covered actions. Bars removal of inspectors general without cause during an impeachment period.

Explanation: Protects people who speak up. Keeps independent watchdogs in place.

SEC. 504. Daily civil penalties for subpoena non compliance.

If any person fails to comply with a subpoena issued by the House of Representatives or its committees regarding matters covered by this Act, a daily civil penalty begins on the day after the return date and continues until substantial compliance or a court finds a lawful excuse.

  • Amount: Up to twenty five thousand dollars per day for senior executive officials and corporate entities. Up to five thousand dollars per day for other witnesses. Amounts set by the issuing committee. Subject to court review for reasonableness.
  • Due process: Written notice. An opportunity to cure. A fast application to the United States District Court for the District of Columbia for enforcement and for a decision on any lawful excuse.
  • Collection: Penalties are debts owed to the United States. The Department of the Treasury collects. Courts may order escrow. Courts may order an asset freeze limited to the penalty amount. Courts may award attorneys’ fees to a prevailing party in the House of Representatives.
Explanation: Creates real time consequences for stonewalling. The longer it drags on the more it costs.

SEC. 505. House civil litigation authorization.

The House of Representatives may bring civil actions in the United States District Court for the District of Columbia to enforce subpoenas, deposition orders, document preservation, and certification requirements under Titles Two through Four. The House may seek a declaration and an order to stop violations. The House may obtain quick consideration.

  • Fast track: Seven day response. Seven day reply. A hearing within ten days. A decision targeted within seven days after the hearing. Direct appeal to the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia.
  • Service of process: Service is effective on delivery to agency counsel or to the Office of Legal Counsel for executive branch defendants.
  • Remedies: Contempt. Daily sanctions to force compliance. Attorneys’ fees for a prevailing party in the House of Representatives.
Explanation: Gives the House a clear and fast lane to court to enforce this law.

TITLE VI — IMPLEMENTATION, BUDGET, SEVERABILITY

SEC. 601. Effective date.

This law takes effect on the day it is signed. Titles Two through Four apply to actions taken during an impeachment period that begins after the law takes effect.

Explanation: Prevents retroactive fights. Focuses on the next impeachment period.

SEC. 602. Authorization of appropriations.

Authorizes the funds that are necessary from fiscal year twenty twenty seven through fiscal year twenty twenty nine for independent oversight, secure archiving, and public dashboards. Offsets come from savings in general government and from recovery of waste.

Explanation: Small and focused funding with pay fors identified.

SEC. 603. Severability and sunset review.

If one part of this law is struck down, the rest stays in effect. The emergency approval pilot in Title Three ends under section 304 unless extended. The Government Accountability Office submits a review at twenty four months with recommendations.

Explanation: Protects the core of the law. Requires a check in to decide what to keep.

Does this block disaster response?

No. Life safety, active disaster response, and court ordered compliance are carved out with short and renewable certifications.

Is this a power grab by Congress?

No. It is time limited. Congress confirms new emergency spending during impeachment while day to day government continues.

Does this freeze all regulations?

No. It pauses only major actions that lack basic certifications and transparency. Routine and safety critical actions continue.

What stops workarounds with acting officials?

Repeat acting appointments and large reorganizations are restricted. Independent offices can flag violations for quick court review.

A BILL

To safeguard continuity of government and public accountability during impeachment. To condition new emergency spending on rapid congressional approval. To require certifications and oversight for major actions. To strengthen records and publication integrity. And for other purposes.

SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE.
This Act may be cited as the “Impeachment Continuity and Accountability Act”.

SECTION 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSES.
(a) Findings. Congress finds that impeachment periods present higher risks of disorder, record loss, and hasty actions. Congress finds that continuity must be preserved. Congress finds that transparent and time limited approval and certification mechanisms protect the public interest.
(b) Purposes. To ensure continuity of government. To prevent abuse of emergency authorities. To protect records and publication integrity. To provide enforceable oversight with quick judicial review.
