Daily Fines for Subpoena Defiance - House Template

Resolution text to establish a daily civil penalty for any person who, without adequate cause, fails to comply with a subpoena issued by the House or any of its committees, after due process checkpoints.

Guidance

  • Use when a witness or officer ignores a valid subpoena after proper service and a reasonable compliance window.
  • Build a record. Include notice, opportunity to cure, and a written determination by the chair.
  • Designate a House officer to assess and collect penalties. Provide for court action to reduce penalties to judgment.
  • Pair with the Wall of Shame card that tracks days late and documents outstanding.

Template Text

Findings

Resolved, That it is the sense of the House that compliance with subpoenas issued under House rules is essential to legislative oversight and the rule of law.

Establishment of Daily Civil Penalty

Sec. 1. A person who, without adequate cause, fails to appear, testify, or produce papers as required by a subpoena issued by the House or any committee or subcommittee shall be liable to the House for a civil penalty of $5,000 for the first day of noncompliance and $10,000 for each day thereafter until compliance or lawful excuse is certified.
Sec. 2. No penalty shall accrue unless the chair certifies in writing that the subpoena was duly authorized and served, that a reasonable time to comply has elapsed, and that the witness was afforded notice and an opportunity to cure.

Assessment and Collection

Sec. 3. The Clerk shall assess penalties upon receipt of the chair certification and shall provide written notice to the person. The Sergeant at Arms is authorized to collect assessed penalties and, upon instruction of the House, to institute civil action to reduce penalties to a judgment in a United States district court.

Safe Harbor

Sec. 4. The chair may suspend accrual upon a showing of substantial compliance, documented negotiations, or a valid privilege assertion supported by a log sufficient to permit evaluation.
